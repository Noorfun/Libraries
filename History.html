History of Libraries in Fairfax County

The history of libraries in Fairfax County dates back to the early 20th century when the first public library was established in 1939. Over the years, the library system has grown significantly, with multiple branches serving the diverse communities of Fairfax County. The libraries have evolved to meet the changing needs of the community, offering not only books but also digital resources, educational programs, and community events. Today, the Fairfax County Public Library continues to be a vital resource for residents, providing access to information, fostering lifelong learning, and promoting community engagement.

Branches, Books, and Beyond

As of May 2015, there are 23 library branches—8 regional branches, 14 community branches, and one that assists people with disabilities. The library also oversees the county's Archives and Records Management Branch. The library's service area spans both the county and Fairfax City and several local jurisdictions through reciprocity agreements, serving nearly half a million registered users. In the library's 2010 fiscal year (through June 2010), nearly 14 million items were borrowed from its collection of nearly three million books, CDs, DVDs, magazines, and other items. Nearly 5.7 million visits were made to the branches, and website visits reached almost 5 million. The library offers a wide variety of services both in the branches and via its website, including searching through its catalog, reserving items, applying for a library card, viewing the calendar of events at libraries, and reserving meeting rooms

Thomas Jefferson Library

▼

The history of this library is a bit unusual and surprising. The Thomas Jefferson Library opened on January 4, 1954 in the rear of the Family Barber Shop in the Graham Road Shopping Center. As it grew, it moved into an apartment in Jefferson Village Apartments. Then, in 1962, a new building was constructed on Arlington Boulevard and was later renovated in 2010. Later, in 2015, the Thomas Jefferson Library completed the Gateway Freedom Project, which had originally started back in 2010, which helped the Vietnamese language collection expand and the engraved brick walkway was completed, to symbolize “gateway to freedom.” This is an example of the many programs and events that the Thomas Jefferson Library has. City of Fairfax Regional Library

▼

This library, one of the first in the Fairfax County Public Library (FCPL), originated in Fairfax, Virginia, beginning as a small cinderblock building in 1935. It underwent its first expansion in 1950, and by 1962, a new library was established at 3915 Chain Bridge Road, known as the Headquarters Library, which opened on July 8, 2026, later renamed Central Library. In 1974, the library introduced the Drive-in Dial-A-Book service, and eventually, it was renamed the Fairfax City Regional Library. Following renovations in 1983, the library relocated temporarily, reopening in 1984 after extensive updates. A groundbreaking for a new City of Fairfax Regional Library occurred in September 2005, leading to the closure of the original 3915 Chain Bridge Road location on November 25, 2007, with the building demolished in 2010. The current City of Fairfax Regional Library opened on January 26, 2008, at 10360 North Street, featuring 44,450 square feet of space designed by David Hallett. Its architecture reflects historical influences, and the Memory Department was added on June 9, 2018.

George Mason Library

▼

During the fall of 1967, George Mason College of the University of Virginia opened its fifth building on the Fairfax Campus. The original four – North (now Finley), South (now Krug), East and West – went into service in August of 1964. A significant part of West Building was used as the college library until it was a finished 14,000 square-foot two-story library in 1967. During the building’s dedication in December, it was named for a local member of the state legislature, Senator Charles Rogers Fenwick. Fenwick, a speaker at the event, was unaware of the naming plan until it was announced at the ceremony itself. He was admittedly surprised and humbled by the gesture. As Mason became an independent university, and enrollment tripled over the next sixteen years, Fenwick would undergo two major additions. A tower was added to the southwest part of the building in 1974, and an identical one on the southeast side in 1983.By the early 2000s library administration and staff were planning again for a new addition to Fenwick that would add more space for study, programming, and housing of staff and library resources. By 2013 ground was broken, and construction was underway. The new Fenwick Library would take over two years to construct and add over 2,000 seats for study, a 24-hour café and study space, a state-of-the-art Special Collections Research Center with dedicated space for exhibitions, and dedicated areas that can be used for special library events. The 150,000-square foot addition was completed in January 2016 and complies with LEED silver standards.
